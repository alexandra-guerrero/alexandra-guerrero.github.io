About Me

I am currently a physics PhD candidate at the University of Chicago, working under my advisor, Prof. Daniel Holz. I received my S.B. in physics at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), with a minor in astronomy. I am a gravitational-wave astrophysicist and a member of the Laser Interferometer Gravitational-wave Observatory (LIGO) collaboration. Broadly, I am interested in using gravitational-wave and electromagnetic observations to learn about compact objects, their progenitors, and their environments. I am also interested in gravitational-wave cosmology.

Scroll down to see some of the questions I am interested in, or read more about my research interests here.

How do binary black holes form, grow, and eventually merge?

One of the ways black holes can form is from the collapse of massive stars. These stellar progenitors often have companions, which can interact, transferring mass and influencing each other throughout their lives. These interactions can determine whether a double compact object is formed, what properties they have, and if they eventually merge, producing gravitational waves. The evolution and interactions of these binary systems are complex and uncertain. Often, binary population-synthesis simulations are used and compared to observations to help constrain physics.

What environments do black holes live in?

Black holes can form in many different environments: either in isolation, in a binary, in dense stellar environments such as globular clusters, or in the centers of galaxies. Determining the proportion of black holes that live in each of these environments, and how they interact with their surroundings, is an open area of study.

How can we use gravitational waves to learn about the cosmological universe?

Double compact objects that merge can produce gravitational waves (GWs). These GWs encode information about the binary, such as the masses of the compact objects and the luminosity distance to the merger, and can act as independent probes of cosmology (or "standard sirens"). This luminosity distance, paired with a redshift, can be used to measure the expansion rate of the universe, the Hubble constant. The redshift can be inferred through an electromagnetic counterpart ("bright sirens"), assuming galaxies in the localization volume are probable hosts ("dark sirens"), or through the population distribution of GW sources ("spectral sirens"). Each method can have potential systematics, and it is important to determine the robustness or effects of any uncertainties.
